    <p class="section-kicker">Who I am now</p>
    <h2>A working profile, not a student-era bio.</h2>
    <p>
      I work at the intersection of data systems, analytics, and decision infrastructure. The strongest through-line in my work is practical reliability: taking messy, fragmented, or operationally sensitive inputs and shaping them into something teams can actually trust and use.
    </p>
    <p>
      That shows up differently depending on the setting. Sometimes it looks like GTM data engineering and attribution. Sometimes it looks like KPI governance and Power BI reporting. Sometimes it looks like experimentation systems, retention analysis, or operational workflows close to compliance and logistics.
    </p>
  </article>

  <article class="surface-card" data-reveal>
    <p class="section-kicker">What ties it together</p>
    <h2>Structured ambiguity and careful definitions.</h2>
    <p>
      I tend to be most useful where a problem is important, multi-team, and just messy enough that nobody fully trusts the current process. That is usually a sign that the system needs clearer definitions, more durable data handling, or a better link between analysis and action.
    </p>
    <p>
      My default instinct is to make the logic legible, the workflow sturdier, and the output easier to act on.
    </p>
  </article>
</div>

<div class="two-column">

    <p class="section-kicker">Education and range</p>
    <h2>Why the portfolio cuts across disciplines.</h2>
    <p>
      I studied Data Science and Business Economics at UC San Diego, with a minor in Urban Studies and Planning. That mix is part of why my work does not fit cleanly into a single narrow lane. I am interested in systems that sit between technical implementation and real-world trade-offs.
    </p>
    <p>
      It is also why the site includes warehouse modeling, product measurement, transportation analysis, and policy-adjacent work without those things feeling random to me.
    </p>
  </article>

  <article class="surface-card" data-reveal>
    <p class="section-kicker">Why these topics stay</p>
    <h2>Transportation, planning, economics, and fairness still matter here.</h2>
    <p>
      The earlier version of this site was very explicit about transportation and urbanism, and I am keeping that thread on purpose. Those interests shaped how I think about operations, throughput, public systems, incentives, and the downstream effects of technical decisions.
    </p>
    <p>
      They are not side notes. They are part of the reason I care about trustworthy metrics, practical fairness, and systems that hold up outside of ideal conditions.
    </p>
  </article>
</div>

  <h2>What I try to bring to teams and projects.</h2>
  <ul class="feature-list">
